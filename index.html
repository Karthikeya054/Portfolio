Product Thinking

How I think as
a Product Manager.

The principles I've developed through building and shipping real products.

01
Start with the sharpest pain, not the broadest scope
In UniConnect, I didn't start with a dashboard or a task manager — I started with the one thing that made faculty lose hours every week: bulk email. Solving that single pain drove adoption of everything else. The lesson: nail one thing first, then expand the wedge.
02
User research before requirements
Every product I've built started with interviews, not assumptions. For UniConnect, 40+ conversations revealed that the real problem wasn't feature gaps — it was fragmented communication creating invisible workload. Research surfaces what users can't articulate themselves.
03
Security and UX are not trade-offs
For the QR Attendance system, encoding raw tokens instead of URLs was both a UX decision and a security decision — students wouldn't accidentally open the web app, and the token couldn't be replayed via a browser. Good security often improves the experience, not degrades it.
04
Design for non-technical users without simplifying
University faculty needed a tool with zero learning curve. But "simple UX" doesn't mean stripped functionality — it means smart defaults, one-click launchers, and live feedback that replaces the need to understand what's happening under the hood. Complexity should hide, not disappear.
05
Meet users where they already work
For Makers Conclave, I built the results portal to read from Google Sheets — because coordinators already maintained their data there. Asking them to switch tools would have created adoption friction. The best product insight is often: don't change the user's workflow, extend it.
06
Visibility builds trust faster than any feature
The UniConnect Ops Dashboard wasn't about analytics — it was about accountability. When leadership can see what's happening in real time, they trust the system enough to rely on it. Dashboards and auto-reports are adoption drivers, not nice-to-haves.

UniConnect

Multi-University Operations Platform · Role: Product Manager & Co-founder
🎯 Took a multi-university platform from 0 to 111+ daily users across 18 universities. Replaced fragmented personal Gmails, WhatsApp threads, and shared spreadsheets with a unified system — reducing email campaign time from 3 hours to 5 minutes, and daily reporting from 30 minutes to 2 minutes.

1 · Context & Background

University operations teams across multiple campuses were managing everything through informal channels — personal Gmail accounts for bulk student communication, WhatsApp groups for coordination, and Google Sheets for tracking. With 18 universities and 100+ staff members in scope, this fragmentation was invisible to leadership but devastating in daily execution. My role was to own the product end-to-end: from identifying the real problem through user interviews, to writing the PRD, coordinating development sprints, and measuring post-launch outcomes.

2 · The Problem
  • Bulk email pain: Sending exam-related emails to 500+ students required 2–3 hours of manual work per campaign — no tracking, no delivery confirmation, no resume capability if it crashed mid-send.
  • Invisible daily operations: Department heads had no real-time visibility into task completion, team activity, or individual performance across campuses. Leadership relied on informal status calls.
  • Exam paper creation: Faculty manually assembled question papers from scratch — selecting questions, formatting, assigning Bloom's taxonomy levels — taking 4+ hours per paper.
  • Budget and event tracking: Proposals were tracked via email chains. No structured approval flow, no audit trail, no post-event reconciliation process.
  • Meeting outcomes lost: Decisions made in meetings were captured in personal notes, never surfaced systematically. Follow-up actions slipped through.
3 · My Approach
  • User research first: Conducted 40+ interviews with university staff, faculty, and operations leads across multiple campuses. Mapped pain frequency and severity to prioritize which problem to solve first.
  • Start with highest pain, highest trust: Email automation was the single most universally despised workflow. Solving it well would establish credibility for everything else — a deliberate adoption wedge strategy.
  • Reliability before features: Phase 1 shipped nothing fancy — just reliable email delivery with per-student tracking. No bells. The constraint forced us to get the foundation right.
  • Auto-aggregate, don't ask: Instead of requiring staff to fill yet another form, the Ops Dashboard auto-aggregates data from all modules. The 2-minute daily form is additive context, not the primary data source.
  • Modular isolation: Designed the schema so each university's data is isolated by default — a trade-off that made onboarding slower but prevented data leakage as we scaled to 18 universities.
4 · Solution & Rationale
  • Email Automation Engine: Gmail OAuth, async job queue (BullMQ/Redis) so bulk sends never block the UI, open and acknowledgment tracking per student, resume capability for interrupted runs.
  • Exam Assessment Engine: Question bank with Bloom's taxonomy tagging, 4-set paper auto-generation with deduplication, PDF export — reducing paper creation from 4 hours to 15 minutes.
  • Ops Dashboard: Auto-aggregation across all modules + a 2-minute daily form + automated daily/weekly/monthly reports pushed to leadership. No extra effort from teams — visibility as a byproduct.
  • Smart Sheets (Individual Performance): Auto-generated attendance, marks, and performance sheets per faculty — replacing 6 hours/week of manual data entry per staff member.
  • Budget Proposals: Full lifecycle from draft → approval → post-event actuals with complete audit log. Finance teams can reconcile in hours instead of days.
  • Meeting Intelligence: Calendar sync, transcript processing, AI action item extraction — so meeting outcomes become structured data, not lost notes.
  • RBAC (12+ roles): University-scoped access with invitation and access-request system — critical at 18-university scale to prevent data crossover.

6 · Learnings & Reflection

Solving one critical problem unlocked everything else. Email automation was the highest-friction workflow, and fixing it built trust across teams. Once users saw immediate value, adoption of other modules — dashboard, assessments, budgets — followed naturally.

Adoption depends on trust, not just features. Users only explored additional functionality after the first module proved reliable. Early success mattered more than feature breadth.

Visibility drove more value than automation. While automation reduced effort, leadership valued dashboards and reports more — because they enabled better decision-making and oversight.

What I'd do differently: I underestimated the complexity of university-specific onboarding. Instead of treating each as a one-off setup, I would design configurable onboarding flows earlier to scale faster.

QR Attendance System

1 · Context & Background

Bootcamps were tracking attendance using paper sheets — a process that created reconciliation overhead, led to data entry errors, and made real-time attendance visibility impossible. The goal was to replace this entirely with a QR-based system: each student has a unique QR code, scanner tablets at the venue read them, and attendance is recorded instantly. My role was to own the product requirements, design the security architecture, and oversee delivery and deployment.

2 · The Problem
  • Paper sheets: Manual collection → manual data entry → no real-time visibility. Errors were discovered hours after the session ended.
  • Security concern: A naive QR implementation (encoding a URL) would allow any student to manually visit the URL and mark themselves present without physically attending.
  • Device management: Multiple tablets act as scanners. There was no way to authorize or revoke individual devices, or know which device recorded which entry.
  • iOS incompatibility: After initial deployment, iPads running Safari crashed with a null srcObject error on camera access — blocking the scanner entirely on Apple hardware.
3 · My Approach
  • Security-first token design: Evaluated two approaches — URL-encoded QR (easy to implement, exploitable) vs raw token QR (slightly more complex, secure by design). Chose raw token encoding so QR codes cannot be opened in a browser, removing the replay attack vector entirely.
  • Device pairing model: Designed a setup flow where each scanner tablet receives a unique deviceKey, pairs via its own QR code, and must be explicitly authorized in the admin portal before it can submit attendance — preventing rogue device submissions.
  • Root cause analysis for iOS bug: The camera activation code ran before the video element mounted to the DOM (it was conditionally rendered). Fixed by always mounting the video element, hiding it via CSS when inactive instead of removing it from the DOM — a targeted, minimal fix.
  • Admin tooling as a first-class feature: Treated admin tooling (bulk QR download, CSV export, device management) as equally important as the student-facing scanner — because without good admin tools, the system creates new overhead even while solving old overhead.
4 · Solution & Rationale
  • Secure QR token system: Each student's QR encodes a raw opaque token — not a URL. Scanning opens nothing in a browser. The scanner app validates tokens server-side against the database.
  • Device authorization portal: Admin generates a setup QR per device → tablet scans it → device is registered and linked. Admin can block or re-authorize any device at any time. All attendance records carry device labels, not UUIDs.
  • Bulk QR download: Admin downloads a ZIP of all student QR codes organized by department — so physical distribution is structured and efficient.
  • IST-accurate CSV export: Attendance export applies Asia/Kolkata timezone to all timestamps — audit-ready for India-based bootcamps where UTC timestamps caused confusion.
  • iOS Safari fix: Video element always mounts in the DOM; camera starts by calling getUserMedia on the already-mounted element. Zero crashes post-fix on any tested iOS device.

6 · Learnings & Reflection

The iOS crash was the most instructive part of this project — not because it was hard to fix, but because it revealed how easy it is to miss platform-specific edge cases when you're developing primarily on a non-Apple device. I now treat iOS Safari as a first-class test surface for any web app that touches camera or media APIs. The security design lesson: the "easy" implementation (URL-based QR) would have shipped faster but would have been fundamentally exploitable. The additional hour spent on raw token design eliminated an entire category of risk. Investing in correctness early is almost always cheaper than patching it post-launch.

1 · Context & Background

Makers Conclave is a NIAT hackathon event where students compete across multiple phases — a written test, a project submission phase, and further rounds. As a developer contributing to the event's infrastructure, I was responsible for building a feature that would let students view their results independently. The core constraint: coordinators already managed results in Google Sheets and couldn't be asked to change their workflow or learn a new tool.

2 · The Problem
  • Manual result queries: Students had no self-serve way to check their phase results. Every query went to a coordinator — "Did I qualify Phase 1?", "What was my Phase 2 score?" — creating significant coordination overhead during and after the event.
  • Data fragmentation: Phase 1 (written test) and Phase 2 (project scores with 5 sub-categories and reviewer feedback) lived in separate spreadsheets with no unified student view.
  • Access friction: Any solution requiring login or account creation would see low adoption from students who just wanted a quick answer about their results.
3 · My Approach
  • Zero workflow change for coordinators: The portal reads directly from existing Google Sheets — no data migration, no new tools for coordinators. Any update they make in Sheets reflects in the portal within 1 hour (cache TTL).
  • Zero friction for students: Students enter their NIAT ID — no login, no password, no account. The friction-to-information ratio had to be as close to zero as possible.
  • Embed-first deployment: Built as an iFrame-embeddable portal so it could go live on the existing NIAT website instantly, without any new domain or deployment complexity for the event organizers.
4 · Solution & Rationale
  • NIAT ID lookup: Student enters their ID → portal fetches from Google Sheets API → displays all phase results in one unified view. No authentication required.
  • Unified Phase view: Consolidated Phase 1 (written test pass/fail) and Phase 2 (5 scoring categories with individual scores + reviewer feedback text) into a single student-facing dashboard with visual progress bars.
  • Phase timeline UI: Shows all phases with lock/unlock status — phases not yet reached show unlock requirements so students understand the progression.
  • 1-hour cache + fallback: Results are cached in-memory to avoid hitting Google Sheets API rate limits. Cache fallback ensures the portal stays available even if Sheets is temporarily unreachable.
  • iFrame deployment: The portal runs as a standalone page embeddable via iFrame — coordinators added one line of HTML to the NIAT site and the feature was live.

6 · Learnings & Reflection

This project reinforced a principle I now apply consistently: meet users where they already work. Coordinators had no reason to learn a new data management tool — they'd been using Sheets effectively. Building around their existing workflow instead of replacing it made the feature deployable in days rather than weeks. The zero-login decision was the most impactful UX choice — adoption of a results portal is only as high as its accessibility. Students who needed to create an account would have just asked a coordinator instead. Removing that barrier removed the need for manual queries entirely.

1 · Context & Background

CDU faculty were required to enter student marks into the Onex portal — a legacy institutional system with a deeply tedious UX. For a class of 60 students, the workflow required: log in, navigate 5 nested dropdowns, type 3 mark values per student, confirm a popup, and repeat — 60 times per subject. Each cycle took 2–3 hours and was entirely manual, error-prone, and universally despised. The faculty wanted a solution that required no technical knowledge to run.

2 · The Problem
  • Volume × repetition: 60 students × (5 dropdowns + 3 inputs + 1 confirmation popup) per subject, every exam cycle. A 3-hour block of focused, error-prone work for each faculty member.
  • High error rate: Manual entry across hundreds of cells with zero validation meant marks were frequently entered in the wrong row or under the wrong subject.
  • Non-technical users: Any solution requiring terminal access, configuration files, or developer-level setup would not be adopted. The UX bar was: one double-click and it runs.
  • Interrupted runs: If the automation or the portal crashed mid-session, faculty needed a way to resume from where they stopped — not restart from scratch.
3 · My Approach
  • Constraints-first design: Started from the hardest constraint — non-technical users — and worked backwards. This ruled out any solution requiring command-line interaction, environment setup, or dependency management by the user.
  • Single executable launcher: Packaged the tool with START_MAC and START_WINDOWS launcher scripts — double-click, it opens a browser tab with a simple UI. Zero installation steps visible to the user.
  • Reliability over speed: Added 40ms delays between keystrokes and interactions — not for user experience, but because the Onex portal had race conditions that caused input failures at higher speeds. Reliability was the KPI, not throughput.
  • Resume capability as a requirement: Designed the session state tracker from the start, not as an afterthought. Faculty running a 60-student batch cannot be expected to restart from scratch if anything goes wrong at student 45.
4 · Solution & Rationale
  • CSV upload: Faculty export marks from Excel (their existing workflow), upload directly to the tool — no reformatting required, no column renaming, no template to learn.
  • Browser automation: Handles all Onex portal quirks: Select2 dropdowns (not standard HTML selects), SweetAlert confirmation popups, input race conditions, and nested navigation — the portal's UX complexity is completely hidden from the user.
  • Live progress stream: Server-Sent Events push real-time updates to the UI — faculty watch every student being processed ("Entered marks for Rahul Kumar… ✓") so they feel in control without needing to understand what's happening technically.
  • Resume on restart: The tool tracks which students have been processed in the current session. If interrupted, restarting picks up from the next unprocessed student — no duplicates, no data loss.
  • Mac + Windows launchers: Single double-click executable on both platforms. No terminal, no PATH configuration, no Python environment management. Runs out of the box.

6 · Learnings & Reflection

The most important product decision here was taking the non-technical user constraint seriously from the start. Many developers would have shipped a command-line tool and called it done. I spent a meaningful portion of the half-day build time on the launcher packaging and the live progress UI — things that don't affect the automation logic at all, but entirely determine whether a non-technical faculty member will actually use the tool. The resume capability felt like "nice to have" when I first thought about it — but it turned out to be essential. A 3-hour manual workflow that could fail at any point and require a full restart would not have been trusted. The ability to resume converted a fragile automation into a reliable one. Small reliability features often matter more than additional functionality.
